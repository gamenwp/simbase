Transmission Control Protocol

May 10
Written By Roel van der Meer

The Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) is a protocol that is used for reliable data communications between computers in a packet-switched computer communication network. TCP defines how applications can create channels of communication across a network and also manages how a message is assembled into smaller packets before they are then transmitted over the internet and reassembled in the right order at the destination address. TCP assumes there is an underlying protocol to route the individual packets of data through the network. Most of the time IP is used to do that. Therefore, the combination TCP/IP is commonly used to refer to communications on the Internet. The Internet Protocol (IP) is responsible for the transportation of individual packets of data from one place to another (source to the destination). TCP is responsible for verifying the correct delivery of data from source to destination and adds support to detect errors or lost data and to trigger retransmission until the data is correctly and completely received. 

To create a communication channel across a network, TCP sets up a connection between the source and the destination by opening a communications port on the computer. The combination of IP-address and communications port is called a socket. After the connection is set up, TCP divides the data into smaller segments. These segments are given a sequence number and a checksum. The segments are then transported using the transport protocol. At the destination, the segments are checked for errors. Faulty segments are thrown away, and a retransmission requested. The segments are put in the right order and delivered to the application of the destination computer.
